# How are boxing gloves supposed to work given this flaw?

This is a naive question that has been troubling me. Following is an image of an ideal clenched bare fist. Next, following is an image of a typical boxing glove. So, following is a boxing glove clenched fist. Note how the thumb is poking out. It is painful to look at, and I am assuming that the only thing protecting this thumb from impact is extra padding above knuckles that absorbs the impact before it reaches the thumb. Or, in other words, the extra padding above the knuckles prevents the thumb from poking out. I am asking this question because I did feel some discomfort in my thumb while doing some bag work with a boxing glove, and now am split between shifting to just wraps + MMA gloves that will at least let me naturally clench my fist. What is going on here? Am I missing something? Is the glove supposed to be designed well enough to protect the thumb?

## Answer (score: 5)
Good question. Macaco Branco's answer is excellent from a perspective of a non-boxer. It does a solid job covering much of boxing glove history, design choices (and popular understanding of the reasoning behind them), and effects on modern boxing. As I boxer, I will add my perspective. I have shattered a cyst in my wrist during impact, but never have I broken a thumb. First of all, proper wrapping technique is paramount. No one should ever put on a boxing glove until they know how to properly wrap. I learned that lesson the hard way when I shattered a cyst. Even though I thought I knew what I was doing, my wrapping technique was not sufficient. I recommend learning proper boxing wrapping technique from multiple accomplished experts, and to ask each of them questions when they inevitably tell you their variation is "the best". Never try an MMA wrap technique with a boxing glove. We wrap our hands differently for a reason. Second of all, before you buy gloves, learn about glove sizing and variations. Just like shoes, boxing gloves come in different designs and sizes. It's not just the weight that differentiates one pair of gloves from another. If your hands or wrists are different sizes, you may even need different size glove for each hand. Remember that a glove must correctly fit all your fingers, your thumb, and your wrist. Don't be cheap when it comes to buying gloves. Quality matters. Just like you probably wouldn't play a sport in low-quality shoes, you don't want to box with stiff low-quality gloves or gloves with insufficient padding. A quality pair of boxing gloves costs real money (much more than MMA gloves). Hang old gloves on the wall. I have many pairs of gloves hanging on walls. I will never put them on again. Gloves lose padding after many impacts (and I've heard padding can even just degrade by itself over time if people aren't training enough). Breaking in gloves can be needed. If a glove fits correctly, you should be able to position your thumb effectively to avoid most impacts. Now that you are wrapped and have properly fitting gloves, punching form is key. If someone's thumb is part of the impact, their form needs much improvement. Effective impact angle is critical for delivering a solid blow, as well as for protecting your own hands and wrists. If someone has a swollen or hurt thumb after training or a bout, improving their form is required. Although it may sound obvious, never forget that your thumbs are part of your hands. Occasionally, I will see someone training who has apparently forgotten this important point. You (partially) feel punches in your fingers, not your thumbs. So people tend to focus on their fingers. Somehow, they forget about their thumbs. I call them "flappy thumbs" because they look like their thumbs are flapping all over the place. Thumbs don't leave your fists. Wild punches are the best way to break way to break a thumb (and are rarely effective). You never want to land a punch with one of your thumbs in the way, and a wild punch is perhaps the best way to make that mistake. Train until you don't throw wild punches under pressure.

## Answer (score: 2)
Caveat that I am not a boxer, so all of this is from miscellaneous research while answering questions on this site. The short answer is that modern boxing gloves do restrict thumb movement, in part by design, but you should still be tucking your thumb when striking. Modern Boxing gloves were designed to prevent grappling A fair amount of the modern "Broughton" design is focused on making some of the more illegal techniques like grabbing, fish-hooking, and eye-poking very difficult. The restriction on how you can move your fingers and thumb is very intentional. Most modern boxing gloves still allow some thumb movement While it is difficult to get the thumb to a "proper" position, there still is enough room to tuck it to the side in most gloves. Leaving it hanging out is essentially from people being lazy while punching. There have been designs to keep the thumb in a safer place and I've run into a few places asserting that the various official organizations for professional boxing have changed the rules over time about whether the gloves need to restrict thumb movement and/or enforce a particular position, although other places have stated that it's based more on personal preference of the boxers. I can't find anything more than anecdotes, so I'm not trying to provide any sources there. Boxing gloves are built for a specific type of punching and will change the "ideal" fist shape Wearing gloves has changed how people punch . In the same way that your technique will change whether you're hitting someone with a knife or a sword, hitting technique with gloves is different from barehand technique. The gloves protect your hands in specific ways, restrict your strikes to particular methods, and change the weight that you're moving around. This is essentially one of the major reasons barehand boxing looks so different from modern gloved boxing (that, and handlebar mustaches went out of style for some reason). Barehand hits require you to have a tighter fist, so the thumb position is more important. Broughton-style gloves, it's less important (although, as I noted above, keeping that thumb floating on top of the hand is usually more of a lazy technique thing).
