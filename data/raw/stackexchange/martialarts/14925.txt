I train and spar in boxing, and am an avid follower of professional fights as well. I've heard almost every coach, other boxer, commentators, and so on refer to "Mayweather is has insane speed, but Oscar De La Hoya is more of a power puncher", and things such as "Focus on speed this round, not power" from my coach. I THINK I get what they are saying, but I got an A+ in Physics class and a cursory reminder definition of Power: In physics, power is the amount of energy transferred or converted per unit time. This confuses me, because by that definition, reducing unit time, aka "speeding up", should INCREASE power. That is, power itself is a function of speed (more precisely, of time). Increasing the speed implies decreasing the time and decreasing the time can increase the power because it decreases the denominator. When I "snap"/"pop" my punches out and back, aren't they actually more powerful? If not, could you please elaborate why not? I'm wondering if when these people are saying "power" they actually mean "amount of force behind the punch?" Is power misused here or am I just totally confused?

## Answer (score: 3)
In addition to speed, you can change mass. You can land a punch with just the mass of your hand (a jab), or you can land a punch with all of your body's mass (a haymaker). As mass is resistance to acceleration, you can more easily land a fast punch with less mass behind it. When someone tells you to "focus on speed rather than power", it means you should sacrifice mass to increase speed.

## Answer (score: 1)
Answering the title of your question: Yes, but is not the main and only variable. Power in punching depends on many variables: strength, body weight, technique, body integrity, and of course, speed/acceleration. Answering the body of your question: I think, yes, you are confused, BUT, is not your fault, you are learning boxing and in this case, boxing parlance is not clear for speed, speed of what? In both the phrases you mention: "Mayweather [-is-] has insane speed , but Oscar De La Hoya is more of a power puncher" and "Focus on speed this round, not power", the word "speed" refers more to the punching rate rather than to the actual speed of each independent punch . The dichotomy between speed and power results from the interplay of punching rate and punching strength. Throwing strong punches requires you to use your whole body, using more energy and usually a bit more time in between them, while lighter punches require less whole-body involvement, less energy and less time in-between punches and resetting. In turn, you can throw a higher volume of light punches in succession rather than strong punches through out the round before you tire yourself out. Throwing more punches in succession (let's say combinations of at least three punches with a mean reset time of 2-4 seconds between combinations) will look faster and more dynamic than a slugger who throws one or two punches before taking 5-8 seconds to reset. Which encompasses both attack and defense "speed". More hopefully helpful insights Additionally to the offensive strategy, this speed/power dichotomy is present in defensive strategy, foot work, hands and body positioning. The interplay of these strategies are the foundations of the main boxing styles. Sluggers stay stationary to maximize focus and power; out-boxers move more around to reduce the opponent's impact chances; and in-fighters will transition between fast and strong punches to damage in close range while being slower and throwing weaker defensive punches out of range to recover. Usually, trainers will combine the offensive and defensive strategies/styles into one with novice boxers. As a boxer grows in experience the training focus should turn more style-specific, most of the time turning to the natural predisposition of the fighter (but in some cases like Floyd Mayweather to prevent further injuries). Out-boxers often rely on volume and attrition rather than single strong KO-punches; sluggers don't move so much, they rely on power, positioning, and timing; in-fighters rely on spurts of speed and power to overwhelm their opponents. On actual punch speed. Some of the variables correlating to punch speed are experience and reach. More experienced boxers will punch faster (so not much to do immediately, just keep training). Longer boxers will reach higher speeds (not necessarily reach the target in less time) (1).
