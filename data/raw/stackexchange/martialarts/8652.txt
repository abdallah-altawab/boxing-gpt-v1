## Answer (score: 2)
I don't box, but I do Capoeira, which involves a similar kind of lateral movement both in dodges and in the general movement of the ginga . I, too, am prone to back pain if I'm not careful, but in my case, it's not so much the lateral movement as the lean. If you are leaning forward at the waist when you do the lateral movement, you put additional strain on the muscles of the lower back because they kind of have to curl up in the course of the movement. I wish I could give a more technical description, but that's how it feels for me. Try doing a series of lateral movements with your torso completely upright. See if that provides less strain. Yes, you probably won't be able to maintain that in a sparring match where you have to lean into the punches to reduce your attack profile, but being able to do the movement without pain will help you to break your brain's insistence that the movement causes pain, and you can gradually increase your lean as you get stronger. As a slight follow-up to this, I've realized some years later that my issue is less general tilt (although there is an aspect to that) and more lumbar spine tilt. If I bend at my waist, or along the upper spine (such as the traditional "crouching over" pose), it's not as bad, but I have come to realize I have a bit of a tendency, when leaning forward, to arch my lumbar spine up and back in the process of lifting my head to continue looking forward. I don't know if this might be factoring into your lower back pain, but the general cure for it, for me, is to always be aware of my lower back when leaving forward, generally ensuring a gentle curve forward there if I'm leaning forward, and when I feel like I have to curve up to see my opponent, I consider that I may be leaning too far forward (in general, in Capoeira, an extreme forward lean is being done as part of an active movement rather than the general ginga , so there are other ways to keep one's eyes on the opponent).
