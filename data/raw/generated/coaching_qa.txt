BOXING COACHING Q&A


Q: How do I improve my jab?

A: The jab is the foundation of boxing — every improvement here pays dividends across your entire game.

1. EXTENSION: Fully extend the jab. Many fighters stop 80% of the way. At full extension, your fist rotates palm-down and your shoulder shrugs slightly to protect your chin.

2. SNAP: The power of the jab comes from its snap, not push. Throw it out and pull it back immediately. Practise on the speed bag to develop this rhythm.

3. HIP ENGAGEMENT: Even the jab involves a subtle hip rotation. Push off your rear foot and let that energy flow through your hip and into the punch.

4. RANGE: You should be at jab range — where you can just touch the opponent with a fully extended jab. Most fighters work too close.

5. DECEPTION: Use the jab in combinations, as a range finder, to the body, doubled up, and as a feint. A predictable jab is easily parried.

Drills: 100 jabs on the heavy bag per session (focus on form). 5 rounds of shadowboxing using only the jab. Mitt work where your coach calls 'jab' randomly.

Q: What is the best way to defend against a southpaw?

A: Fighting a southpaw is a tactical puzzle many orthodox fighters never solve.

FOOTWORK FIRST: The key battle is foot position. You want your lead foot (left) on the OUTSIDE of their lead foot (right). This gives you a clear line to their head with your right hand, while their left hand must reach across to hit you. Circle LEFT (to their right) to move off their left hand.

JAB TO THE BODY: Southpaws expose their right side when they jab. A jab to their body is frequently open and hard for them to see.

RIGHT HAND OVER THEIR JAB: When the southpaw jabs, their right side opens. Your cross can catch them coming in.

LEFT HOOK TO THE BODY: Their liver is exposed. A short left hook to the body is a devastating weapon against any southpaw.

AVOID CIRCLING RIGHT: Moving right takes you directly into their left (power) hand.

Q: How do I stop getting hit by the counter right hand?

A: Getting countered after your jab is one of the most common problems in boxing.

1. SLIP THE JAB: When you throw the jab, slip your head slightly right as the jab extends. If their counter right comes, it misses because your head has moved.

2. PULL THE JAB BACK FASTER: The hand should return to guard as fast as it extended. A slow retraction is an invitation to counter.

3. JAB FROM DIFFERENT ANGLES: Mix in jabs to the body, at different distances, while moving laterally. Predictable jabs are countered; unpredictable ones are not.

4. FOLLOW THE JAB: The best defence is not giving them time to counter. If your jab lands, immediately follow with the cross (the 1-2).

5. LEAD WITH THE SHOULDER: Slightly rolling the shoulder forward with the jab protects the chin and narrows the target.

Q: How do I develop knockout power?

A: Knockout power comes from mechanics, genetics, timing, and conditioning.

MECHANICS (most trainable):
- Hip rotation is the primary source of power. Every hard punch rotates from the feet up: foot pivot, knee drive, hip rotation, shoulder rotation, arm extension.
- Relax before impact: tense muscles slow the punch. Stay relaxed and contract at the moment of impact (like cracking a whip).
- Land with the first two knuckles (index and middle finger).

TIMING:
- Punching an opponent who is moving INTO your punch doubles the effective force. This is why counters produce knockouts.

CONDITIONING:
- Core rotation: medicine ball rotational throws, cable rotations.
- Hip power: deadlifts, squats, kettlebell swings.

Fix mechanics first. Poor mechanics prevent every fighter from reaching their ceiling.

Q: What is the best training schedule for an amateur boxer preparing for a tournament?

A: 12-week tournament preparation:

WEEKS 1-4 (General Preparation):
Roadwork: 4-5 mornings/week, 30-40 min easy run + 6x200m sprints twice/week.
Gym: 5 sessions — shadowboxing, heavy bag, mitts, speed bag, double-end bag, light sparring.
Strength: 2x/week compound lifts (squat, deadlift, press).

WEEKS 5-8 (Specific Preparation):
Increase sparring to 6-8 rounds, 3x/week. Sprint work replaces easy running.
Reduce heavy lifting — switch to explosive (med ball, plyometrics).

WEEKS 9-10 (Peak):
Maintain intensity, reduce volume 20%. Sparring: 2x/week, sharp.

WEEKS 11-12 (Taper):
Light technical work only. No new techniques. Trust the preparation.
Sleep 8-9 hours. You cannot get fitter in the final week — only more tired.

Q: How do I improve head movement in boxing?

A: Head movement must be trained systematically.

THE KEY: Head movement originates from the knees and hips, not the neck. Bend your knees and you move your entire body. Bend only your neck and you move a few inches.

THE FOUR CORE MOVEMENTS:
1. Slip outside (torso rotates, head moves outside the punch)
2. Slip inside (head moves inside — riskier, creates hook/uppercut counters)
3. Pull back (lean away — simplest, useful from range)
4. Roll under (U-shape under hooks — knees not neck)

TRAINING:
- Slip rope: hang a rope at head height, practise slipping side to side.
- Double-end bag: forces head movement or you get hit on the return.
- Dedicated sparring: partner only throws jabs, you only slip and counter.

ELITE HABIT: Move your head BEFORE and DURING your own punches. Every combination should end with a defensive movement — not standing still.

Q: What is the liver shot and how do I set it up?

A: The liver shot is one of the most devastating punches in boxing.

ANATOMY: The liver is under the right side of the ribcage (your left side when facing the opponent). When struck cleanly, it causes intense wave-like pain that is nearly impossible to fight through.

THE PUNCH: A left hook to the body, thrown slightly upward to get under the elbow and rib cage.

SETUPS:
1. 1-2-3 to body: Jab head, cross head, left hook BODY. First two punches raise the guard.

2. FEINT HIGH: Fake a jab to the head. As the guard comes up, attack the exposed body.

3. ACCUMULATE: Throw body shots consistently from round 1. The liver bruises progressively — a tap in round 8 hurts more than a hard shot in round 1.

4. CATCH THE CROSS: When they rotate into their cross, their right side opens. Time a short left hook to the body to catch the rotation.

Never telegraph the body shot. The setup punch must genuinely threaten the head.

Q: How do I use the jab to control distance?

A: Distance management is the jab's most important role beyond scoring.

THE RANGE CONCEPT: You want to be at a distance where your fully extended jab just reaches the opponent's face. At this range you can reach them; they cannot reach you without stepping in first — giving you time to react.

USING THE JAB TO ESTABLISH RANGE:
- Use a measuring jab (pawing jab) to gauge distance as you enter and exit.
- If the opponent is too close, push the jab into them to create space while simultaneously stepping back with the rear foot.
- If the opponent circles away, step-drag to close distance, then fire the jab.

CONTROL PATTERNS:
- Double jab + reset: two quick jabs then step back to reset range.
- Jab + pivot: jab and pivot 45 degrees to a new angle. They must reset; you don't.
- Body jab: shifts their guard down, opens the head, and tests their reaction.

KEY: The opponent should always be reacting to your jab. If they are setting the pace, your jab is not working hard enough.

Q: How should I warm up before sparring?

A: A proper warm-up before sparring reduces injury risk and improves performance.

PHASE 1 — GENERAL WARM-UP (10-15 min):
3 rounds of jump rope at easy to moderate pace. This raises core temperature and activates the cardiovascular system.

PHASE 2 — MOBILITY (5-10 min):
Neck rolls, shoulder circles, hip circles, knee circles, ankle rolls. Dynamic stretches: leg swings, arm circles, torso rotations. Do NOT do static stretching before sparring — it reduces explosive output.

PHASE 3 — BOXING-SPECIFIC ACTIVATION (10-15 min):
2 rounds of easy shadowboxing focusing on footwork and defence. 1-2 rounds of light bag work to groove technique. Brief mitt work to sharpen reflexes.

PHASE 4 — MENTAL PREPARATION:
Review your game plan. Identify the specific technique or tactic you will work on in this sparring session. Purposeful sparring beats random sparring every time.

NOTE: Spar within 15-20 minutes of completing the warm-up. If you wait longer, your body will cool down and you have to warm up again.
